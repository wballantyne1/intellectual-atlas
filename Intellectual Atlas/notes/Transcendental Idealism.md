# Transcendental Idealism
`idea` | First articulated: 1781 | Contested: yes
**Traditions:** Critical Philosophy · German Idealism · Epistemology
**Primary thinker:** [[Immanuel Kant]]

---

## Summary
Kant's doctrine that space and time are not features of reality as it is in itself, but forms the human mind imposes on experience. We can only ever know the world as it appears to us (phenomena), never as it really is (things-in-themselves). This was Kant's attempt to explain how objective knowledge is possible while accepting that we never have direct access to mind-independent reality.

---

## Connections
- Developed by: [[Immanuel Kant]] in [[Critique of Pure Reason]]
- Extended by: [[Johann Gottlieb Fichte]]
- Contradicted by: [[Georg Wilhelm Friedrich Hegel]] — who dissolved the phenomena/noumena distinction entirely
- Extended by: [[Arthur Schopenhauer]] — who reinterpreted the thing-in-itself as Will
