# The Floating Man

**c. 1020 CE | Avicenna**

[[Avicenna]]'s most celebrated thought experiment — and one of the most prescient arguments in the history of philosophy.

Imagine a person who comes into existence fully formed, suspended in empty air. They cannot see (blindfolded), cannot hear, cannot feel their own body or limbs. Zero sensory input. Would this person know they exist?

Avicenna says: **yes**. The awareness of one's own existence is immediate and requires no external evidence whatsoever. You cannot think your way out of self-awareness.

## Why it matters

The argument is doing two things at once:

**First**, it separates the soul from the body. If you can know you exist without any input from your body or senses, then the self cannot be *just* the body. The soul is a distinct, irreducible thing.

**Second**, it establishes self-awareness as the most fundamental fact of consciousness — prior to all perception, prior to all knowledge of the external world.

Avicenna got here around 1020 CE. Descartes got to the same place — *cogito ergo sum* (I think, therefore I am) — in 1637. The gap is 600 years.

The argument has renewed relevance in modern philosophy of mind, where it connects to debates about qualia, first-person experience, and whether consciousness can be reduced to physical processes.
