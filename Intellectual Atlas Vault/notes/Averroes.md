# Averroes

**1126–1198 CE | Arab Andalusian | Córdoba**

Known in medieval Europe simply as "The Commentator" — with Aristotle understood to be "The Philosopher." Averroes, known in Arabic as Ibn Rushd, devoted his life to recovering what he believed was the authentic Aristotle, stripped of the Neoplatonist additions introduced by [[Al-Kindi]] and [[Avicenna]].

He worked in Córdoba and Marrakesh, at the western edge of the Islamic world — and it was from there that his Latin translations swept into Christian Europe, igniting the Scholastic movement and directly provoking [[Thomas Aquinas]].

## Core ideas

**Pure Aristotelianism** — Averroes argued that [[Avicenna]] had distorted Aristotle by mixing in Neoplatonist ideas. He wanted to return to Aristotle's actual texts and let them speak without contamination. He wrote three tiers of commentary on virtually every Aristotelian work: short summaries, medium paraphrases, and long detailed analyses.

**[[Active Intellect]] (the dangerous version)** — Averroes took the most radical position on Aristotle's active intellect: it is a single, universal intellect shared by all of humanity, not a faculty of the individual soul. The implication? There is no personal immortality. You do not survive death as an individual. When your body dies, your particular mind dissolves back into the universal intellect.

This view — called Averroism — caused a crisis when it reached Paris. If Averroes was right, Christian theology about heaven and personal resurrection was wrong. Thomas Aquinas wrote major works specifically to refute it.

**Reason and faith** — In *On the Harmony of Religion and Philosophy*, Averroes argued that philosophy and religion address the same truth but at different levels: religion for everyone, philosophy for those trained to use reason. This was a more radical separation than Al-Kindi's harmonisation.

## Key works

- *The Incoherence of the Incoherence* (1180) — a defence of philosophy against Al-Ghazali's attacks
- *Commentaries on Aristotle* (c. 1169–1195) — the life's work
- *On the Harmony of Religion and Philosophy* (c. 1190)
