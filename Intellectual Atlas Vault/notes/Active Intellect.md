# Active Intellect

**c. 350 BCE onwards | Originated with Aristotle**

One of the most disputed ideas in the history of philosophy — and the concept that, when Averroes reinterpreted it, nearly destroyed Christian theology's account of personal immortality.

[[Aristotle]] distinguished between the *passive intellect* (the mind's capacity to receive and store knowledge) and the *active intellect* (the power that makes thinking actually happen). His analogy: the passive intellect is like a surface that can be written on; the active intellect is the light that lets you see to write. But he left tantalizingly unclear whether this active intellect was part of the individual soul or something external.

## Three interpretations

**[[Al-Kindi]]** — the active intellect is God, the First Cause that illuminates all human thinking.

**[[Avicenna]]** — the active intellect is the lowest of ten cosmic intelligences emanating from God. A separate, eternal entity that "gives forms" to human minds — like a cosmic radio signal that individual minds tune into.

**[[Averroes]]** — the most radical reading: the active intellect is a *single*, *universal* intellect shared by all humanity. It is not individual. When you think, you temporarily participate in this universal mind. When you die, your individual participation ends — there is no personal immortality. The idea you had lives on in the universal intellect; *you* do not.

This last interpretation caused a crisis in Christian Europe. [[Thomas Aquinas]] (stub) wrote his *Summa Theologica* partly to refute it — if Averroes was right, personal resurrection was philosophically impossible.
