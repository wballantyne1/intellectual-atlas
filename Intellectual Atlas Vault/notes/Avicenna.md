# Avicenna

**980–1037 CE | Persian | Bukhara**

The greatest philosopher-scientist of the medieval world. Avicenna — known in Arabic as Ibn Sina — produced the largest encyclopaedia of knowledge written by a single person in the medieval period, covering philosophy, logic, mathematics, and medicine. He did this while serving as a court physician and fleeing political upheaval across Persia.

He extended [[Al-Kindi]]'s Aristotelian-Neoplatonist synthesis into a complete, systematic philosophy — and then kept going into medicine, producing a medical encyclopaedia that European universities used for six centuries.

## Core ideas

**Essence and existence** — Avicenna drew a sharp distinction between what a thing *is* (its essence) and the fact that it *exists*. A unicorn has an essence (we know what one would be) but does not exist. God, uniquely, is the being whose essence *is* existence — the only entity that cannot not exist. This argument was enormously influential on both Islamic and Christian scholastic philosophy.

**[[The Floating Man]]** — his most celebrated thought experiment. Imagine a person created fully formed, suspended in empty space, unable to see, hear, or feel anything — not even their own limbs. Would this person know they exist? Avicenna says yes: self-awareness is immediate and irreducible, proof that the soul is not just the body. He got there 600 years before Descartes said *cogito ergo sum*.

**[[Active Intellect]]** — Avicenna described ten cosmic intelligences emanating from God, the lowest of which illuminates human thinking. This Neoplatonist addition to Aristotle was precisely what [[Averroes]] would later attack.

## Key works

- *The Book of Healing (Kitab al-Shifa)* (1027) — a vast encyclopaedia of philosophy and science
- *The Canon of Medicine* (1025) — the standard medical reference in Europe until the 1700s
- *On the Soul* (c. 1014) — contains the Floating Man argument
