# Al-Kindi

**c. 801–873 CE | Arab | Baghdad**

The first self-identified Arab philosopher, and the architect of the Translation Movement. Al-Kindi worked at Baghdad's House of Wisdom under Abbasid patronage, directing the systematic rendering of Greek philosophy into Arabic — and then building his own philosophy on top of it.

Think of him as the person who opened the door. Before Al-Kindi, Greek thought was largely inaccessible to the Islamic world. After him, Aristotle, Plato, and Plotinus were all available in Arabic — and the arguments about what they meant could begin.

## Core ideas

His most important claim was that philosophy and Islamic faith are compatible. At the time, this was not obvious: many religious scholars viewed Greek philosophy as pagan and dangerous. Al-Kindi argued that reason and revelation both lead toward the same truth, just by different routes. This single move made the entire Islamic philosophical tradition possible.

His metaphysics drew on [[Aristotle]]'s First Cause and [[Plotinus]]'s concept of [[The One]], which he identified with Allah. From this single divine source, all things emanate — a framework inherited by [[Avicenna]] and [[Averroes]] after him.

## Key works

- *On First Philosophy* (c. 830) — his most systematic philosophical work
- *On the Intellect* (c. 835) — his treatment of the [[Active Intellect]]
- *On the Definitions of Things* — a philosophical dictionary, the first of its kind in Arabic
