# Islamic Golden Age

**c. 750–1258 CE | Abbasid Caliphate, Persia, Al-Andalus**

The period of extraordinary intellectual flourishing across the Islamic world that preserved, extended, and transmitted Greek philosophy to medieval Europe. Without it, most of Aristotle would have been permanently lost.

Think of the Islamic Golden Age as a relay runner in the middle of a very long race. Greek philosophy was passed to Islamic scholars in the 8th century; 500 years later, they passed it — transformed and greatly enriched — to Christian Europe. The baton never hit the ground.

## What happened

The Abbasid Caliph al-Mansur founded Baghdad in 762 CE and established the House of Wisdom (*Bayt al-Hikma*) — a translation bureau and library that became the intellectual capital of the world. [[The Translation Movement]] systematically rendered Aristotle, Plato, Galen, Euclid, and Ptolemy into Arabic.

This was not passive archiving. Scholars actively debated, critiqued, and extended what they translated. [[Al-Kindi]] showed that Greek philosophy was compatible with Islam. [[Avicenna]] built the largest philosophical system of the medieval world. [[Averroes]] produced definitive commentaries on Aristotle that Christian Europe would use for centuries.

The tradition also flourished in Al-Andalus (Islamic Spain), creating a second centre of learning in Córdoba. When Christian scholars came into contact with this material — through the Crusades and the Reconquista — Latin translations flooded into European universities, directly sparking the Scholastic movement.

The tradition was effectively ended by the Mongol sack of Baghdad in 1258.

## Key contributions

- Systematic translation and preservation of Greek philosophy
- Reconciliation of Greek reason with monotheistic theology
- Avicennian metaphysics: essence vs. existence
- Averroist Aristotelianism transmitted to Christian Europe
- Major advances in medicine, mathematics, optics, and astronomy
